I am a designer. I have built [Glowtris](https://glowtris.com) — a browser Tetris game — almost entirely by speaking with Claude. I cannot code. I have never been able to.

And yet, I built it.

This blog is simply my documentation of what happened. The design decisions that worked. The engineering problems I encountered and how we resolved them. The mistakes I made. The fortunate guesses that turned out to be wise.

## What you will find here

**The real problems I encountered**

This is not a tutorial or a guide to best practices. Rather, it is my honest account: "I attempted X, it failed completely, and here is what I learned." Specific problems with concrete solutions. Sometimes in hindsight, the solution was obvious. Sometimes, I was simply fortunate.

**Engineering discoveries**

Fixed timesteps. Why setTimeout becomes a trap in game development. Redis date-keyed buckets. Vercel's read-only filesystem. These are the insights you gain when building something without truly understanding what you are doing.

**The design process**

How I described color systems in my prompts. What made the neon glow actually function. Why an emoji-first UI is not merely aesthetic—it is genuinely clearer. What worked well and what required iteration.

**Infrastructure lessons**

CI/CD pipeline mistakes. GitHub Actions challenges. Scaling issues. The problems that surface after launch, not before.

## The sincere part

I am not an expert in any of these areas. I am a designer with seven years of visual design experience, but zero years in game development. I do not know TypeScript best practices. I cannot explain async/await without looking it up.

What I can do is this: observe what Claude has created, assess whether it is correct or incorrect, and articulate what needs to change. My eye is the filter. The AI provides the hands.

That is all. This blog is my account of what that process genuinely looks like.
