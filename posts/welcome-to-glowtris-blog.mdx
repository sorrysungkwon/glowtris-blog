I'm a designer. I've worked for 7 years—in Seoul, in New York. I understand color theory, visual hierarchy, typography. But coding? That's different. I don't understand pointers or why `npm install` sometimes just doesn't work. I can only guess.

Yet I built [Glowtris](https://glowtris.com). A full browser game. Almost entirely by talking to Claude.

I can't code. Never could. And here's the weird part—that turned out to be exactly the skill I needed.

## What happened in a month

I asked Claude: "Can we build a Tetris game that runs in a browser?"

Claude said yes. So we did. A week for the core engine. Another week fixing the stuff that broke. Another for design, branding, infrastructure. Thirty days, and I had a shipping game.

My job was simple: look at what Claude built. Tell it if it's right or wrong. Describe what needs to change. That's it.

Claude was the hands. I was the eye.

## This blog is a record

Not a tutorial. Not best practices. Just: "I tried X, it broke completely, here's what I learned."

You'll find real problems I hit. The design decisions that actually worked. The engineering disasters and how we got past them. PixiJS on Safari becoming a special kind of hell. Redis buckets becoming surprisingly elegant. Vercel's read-only filesystem becoming a weird blessing in disguise.

And mostly—how I had no idea what I was doing, still don't, but built something anyway.

## Who this is for

**Designers who want to code**

You feel stuck. You've felt it for years. You think coding is for people who understand recursion. It's not. I don't understand recursion. I asked Claude what it was, he explained it once, and I've been fine. You don't need to understand everything. You need to be able to describe what you want.

**People building things that keep breaking**

There's no magic here. Just specific problems with specific fixes. Problems you probably hit too. Maybe our solutions will help. Maybe they'll just make you feel less alone.

**People who thought they were alone**

I built this in a military barracks. At night, in my bunk, with my laptop. No one around me knew what I was doing. No one checked on my progress. But I kept going anyway. You might be somewhere like that too. This is for you.

## Why I'm writing this

Because building something is hard. And doing it without knowing what you're doing is harder. And doing it alone is the hardest part.

But it's not impossible. I'm living proof that it's not.

Every post here is something I actually built, actually struggled with, actually fixed. I'm writing it down with care because maybe you'll read it and think: "Oh good, I'm not the only one who got stuck there."

That's the whole point of this blog.
