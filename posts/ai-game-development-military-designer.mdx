I am a designer with 7 years of experience. I've hustled through New York and Seoul, perfectly mastering color theory and typography along the way.

But I am absolutely hopeless at coding. I have no idea what a pointer is, I can't comprehend `async/await`, and I am completely clueless as to why `npm install` blows up randomly.

Yet, I built an entire browser-based Tetris game, **[Glowtris](https://glowtris.com)**, completely from scratch using Claude.

About three weeks ago, completely sick of the monotony of military life, I opened Claude during my late-night study time and bluntly asked:
*"Can you build a Tetris game that runs in the browser?"*

Claude zoned out for 20 minutes, and then suddenly spat out **something that actually worked.**

- One week to build the game core and stand up a server.
- One week to post it on Reddit, get utterly destroyed by feedback, and fix bugs.
- One week for design, branding, and infrastructure setup.

In just a month, a fully deployable game was suddenly staring back at me. My role in all of this was dead simple: look at the code Claude spit out and point fingers saying, "This is broken, that color looks weird."

Claude was the hands, and I was the eyes.

## 🔥 Vibe Coding: Between Miracles and Disasters

Unfortunately, this blog isn't a friendly tutorial or an answer sheet.

Instead, it's closer to an **incorrect answer notebook** documenting "I tried this, it completely blew up, and I stayed up for days barely managing to fix it."

Here’s what you’ll find on this blog:
- The spectacular death of my attempt to migrate the game engine to PixiJS.
- The totally hacked-together, yet brilliantly functional, Redis bucket workaround for my leaderboards.
- The time I built a blog without understanding Vercel's read-only filesystem and had to nuke it entirely.

## 💡 Who the Hell Should Read This Dumpster Fire?

**1. Designers who want a taste of coding**
Vibe coding doesn't require you to understand 100% of the code. You just need a functioning mouth to accurately describe "what you want."

**2. People whose servers crash every time they try to build something**
A magical world where AI codes everything for you? Dream on. Ultimately, human beings have to face specific, nasty errors. If my documented disasters save you even one day of banging your head against the wall, I'll call that a success.

**3. Those who feel lonely coding by themselves**
Enlisting in the military late in life, I spent my late-night study time staring blankly at a monitor in the cyber-knowledge room. Nobody asked what I was building. It's lonely and daunting, but I kept building anyway. If you're in a similar situation, I hope this blog offers a small comfort.

## 🏃‍♂️ Why I Keep Building

Building things when you don't know what you're doing is brutally hard. Doing it alone is even harder.
But it turns out, it's not impossible.

I hope you look at my trial and error and chuckle, thinking, "Oh? He got stuck there too? Guess I'm not the only idiot."

Because even a coding-hopeless designer like me pulled it off. You can do it too.
