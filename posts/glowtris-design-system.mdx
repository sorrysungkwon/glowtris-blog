I'm a designer by training and instinct. I understand color theory, visual hierarchy, negative space, typography. I do not understand pointers, async/await, or why `npm install` sometimes just... doesn't work.

But I've been building Glowtris — a browser Tetris game — almost entirely alone. This post is about how that actually works when you're a designer who vibe-codes.

## What "vibe-coding" means in practice

People use "vibe-coding" to mean different things. For me it means: I know what I want. I describe it. The AI builds it. Then I look at what it built, compare it to what I had in my head, and tell it what's wrong.

The key insight is that my eye — trained by years of design work — is still the quality filter. The AI does the labor. I do the judgment.

It's not magic. It's more like having a very fast junior dev who needs constant art direction.

## The Glowtris color system

I started with a constraint: it had to feel like a neon arcade game from the future, but also be readable and not exhausting to look at for 20 minutes.

My first prompt was something like:

> *"Make the background very dark navy, almost black. The neon glow colors for the pieces should be cyan, magenta, yellow, and green. The UI should feel like a dark interface from a sci-fi movie — clean, minimal, glowing."*

Claude gave me CSS with `#000000` for the background and some rough neon colors. The problem: pure black kills the glow effect. Glow looks best against dark *blue-black*, not neutral black. I had to push on that:

> *"The background is too flat. It should be a very dark desaturated navy — think #04041e or similar. The glow on the pieces should look like they're actually emitting light."*

That second pass was much closer. The `#04041e` I mentioned off the top of my head ended up being the actual production value. Sometimes you just know.

## The typography problem

The logo font is Orbitron — that heavy, geometric sci-fi typeface. It reads as "space game" from a hundred meters away, which is exactly what I wanted.

But Orbitron at body copy sizes is unreadable. So the content needed a completely different voice.

I wanted to use Pretendard for Korean text because it's the cleanest system-neutral Korean sans-serif I know. For English body text, I wanted Inter or the system stack. I told Claude:

> *"Header and logo: Orbitron. Body and UI: Pretendard for Korean, system-ui for English. Monospace elements like category tags: JetBrains Mono."*

The three-font system — display / body / mono — is a standard design pattern that creates hierarchy without visual noise. Claude implemented it correctly because the pattern is well-established enough to be in its training data. When I ask for something standard, it usually gets it right.

## Light mode was an afterthought that became the default

The original Glowtris aesthetic is dark. The whole brand is built around glow-on-dark. So the blog started dark too.

Then I looked at it on my phone at noon and it was fine, but I felt like the blog deserved to be legible in daylight conditions without the glow-heavy aesthetic getting in the way of reading.

The prompt was simple:

> *"글로우트리스 블로그는 디폴트가 라이트모드, 그리고 다크모드가 옵션, 선택할수있게해줘. 그리고 톤은 완전 테크블로그느낌으로."*

("Make the Glowtris blog default to light mode, with dark mode as an option. Make it feel like a tech blog.")

The challenge was making light mode work with a brand built for dark backgrounds. My solution: in light mode, the glow colors get replaced by proper blue (`#2563eb`), which is confident and techy without needing a dark surface. The dot-grid hero background came from Vercel's website — that pattern is everywhere on modern tech blogs because it implies "infrastructure" without being literal about it.

Dark mode keeps the original `#00c8ff` cyan and the dark navy. It's the same brand, just the alternate colorway.

## What I had to fix manually

Not everything comes out right from prompting. The things I consistently have to fix:

**Spacing.** AI-generated CSS spacing is usually either too tight or too generous. I almost always tighten the letter-spacing on the logo text, add more breathing room around section dividers, and reduce padding in cards. My mental model for "right" spacing is calibrated from years of looking at designed products.

**Typography scale.** The default type scale suggestions are usually geometrically correct but visually inert. I always push heading sizes up and body sizes down slightly to create more contrast between levels.

**Color relationships.** The AI can match a single color correctly, but often struggles with how colors *interact* across contexts. The active filter button needing inverted text color in light vs dark mode — that required me to specify it explicitly, twice.

**Microinteractions.** The hover states and transitions are always too subtle or too flashy by default. I want hover effects that are quick and confident, not slow and dramatic. "0.15s ease, no scale transform" is something I end up writing in almost every project.

## The design tool of the future is your mouth

I have opinions about every pixel. Working with Claude means I can express those opinions in plain language and have them rendered immediately. The iteration speed is genuinely unprecedented.

What I can't prompt my way around is the judgment. Knowing that `#04041e` is better than `#000000` for glow effects, knowing that the dot-grid pattern says "tech blog" without screaming it, knowing that three-font systems need clear rules about which font goes where — that's experience, not automation.

The AI does the building. The designer still has to know what to build.
